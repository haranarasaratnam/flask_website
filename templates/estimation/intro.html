Bayesian Filtering

Introduction

A dynamic state-space model in discrete time can be written as a set of nonlinear equations: \[ \begin{align} \text{Process equation: } \mathbf{x}_{k} &= \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}_{k-1}, \mathbf{u}_{k}, \mathbf{v}_{k}) \\ \text{Measurement equation: } \mathbf{z}_{k} &= \mathbf{h}(\mathbf{x}_{k}, \mathbf{w}_{k}) \end{align} \] where \(\mathbf{x}_{k}\in R^{n}\) is the state vector at time \(k\), \(\mathbf{u}_{k}\in R^{p}\) is the control input at time \(k\), \(\mathbf{z}_{k}\in R^{d}\) is the measurement taken at time \(k\), \(\mathbf{v}_{k}\) is the process noise, and \(\mathbf{w}_{k}\) is the measurement noise.

Bayesian filter theory asks how we can recursively estimate the hidden state \(\mathbf{x}_{k}\) given the noisy measurements and inputs up to time \(k\), \(D_{k}=\{\mathbf{z}_{1},\mathbf{z}_{2}\ldots\mathbf{z}_{k}, \mathbf{u}_{1},\mathbf{u}_{2}\ldots\mathbf{u}_{k}\}\).

Conceptual recursive solution

The conceptual solution has two stages. The time update propagates the previous posterior forward through the process model: \[ p(\mathbf{x}_{k}|D_{k-1}, \mathbf{u}_{k}) = \int_{R^{n_{x}}}p(\mathbf{x}_{k-1}|D_{k-1})p(\mathbf{x}_{k}|\mathbf{x}_{k-1}, \mathbf{u}_{k})d\mathbf{x}_{k-1}. \]

The measurement update then folds in the new observation via Bayes' rule: \[ p(\mathbf{x}_{k}|D_{k}) = \frac{1}{c_{k}} p(\mathbf{x}_{k}|D_{k-1}, \mathbf{u}_{k})p(\mathbf{z}_{k}|\mathbf{x}_{k}, \mathbf{u}_{k}), \] where the normalising constant is \[ \begin{align} c_{k} &= p(\mathbf{x}_{k}|D_{k-1}, \mathbf{u}_{k}) \\ &= \int_{R^{n_{x}}}p(\mathbf{x}_{k}|D_{k-1}, \mathbf{u}_{k})p(\mathbf{z}_{k}|\mathbf{x}_{k}, \mathbf{u}_{k})d\mathbf{x}_{k}, \end{align} \] and \(n_{x}\) is the dimension of the state vector. These integrals are typically intractable for nonlinear systems.

Approximate solutions to nonlinear systems

The Kalman filter is the celebrated optimal solution to the linear Gaussian case, but no such optimal solution exists for general nonlinear systems. Researchers have historically taken two approaches to derive approximate solutions:

  • Kalman-like (innovations-based) algorithms: Extended Kalman Filter (EKF), Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF), Central-Difference Kalman Filter (CDKF), Gauss–Hermite Quadrature Kalman Filter (GH-QKF).
  • Moment-closing algorithms: Kushner's nonlinear filter, grid filters, particle filters.

Each of these filters trades off accuracy against computational cost — or pays a price in both. In the next section we introduce the Cubature Kalman Filter (CKF), a new Bayesian nonlinear filter derived with mathematical rigour, and we show that it yields reasonably accurate estimates at minimal cost.
