Estimating States of a DC Motor

Measurement Noise Variance for Angular Velocity

1. Understanding the Encoder's Measurement Noise

A 12-bit encoder provides discrete measurements of the angle (θ) with a resolution determined by the total number of counts:

Resolution: \( \frac{360^\circ}{2^{12}} = \frac{360^\circ}{4096} \approx 0.0879^\circ \ (\text{per count}) \)

The quantization error introduced by the encoder is uniformly distributed over \( [-\frac{\text{Resolution}}{2}, \frac{\text{Resolution}}{2}] \). The variance of this uniform distribution is:

\( \sigma_\theta^2 = \frac{\text{Resolution}^2}{12} \)

This variance represents the noise variance of the angular position measurements.

2. Relationship Between Angular Position and Velocity

Angular velocity (ω) is the time derivative of the angular position (θ):

\( \omega = \frac{d\theta}{dt} \)

For discrete measurements from the encoder, angular velocity is approximated as the finite difference:

\( \omega \approx \frac{\Delta \theta}{\Delta t} \)

where \( \Delta \theta = \theta_k - \theta_{k-1} \) is the difference between consecutive encoder measurements, and \( \Delta t \) is the sampling interval.

3. Noise Variance in Angular Velocity

The noise variance in ω depends on the noise variance in θ and the sampling interval \( \Delta t \). Assuming \( \Delta \theta \) noise is uncorrelated, the variance of ω can be approximated as:

\( \sigma_\omega^2 \approx \frac{2 \sigma_\theta^2}{(\Delta t)^2} \)

Substituting \( \sigma_\theta^2 = \frac{\text{Resolution}^2}{12} \):

\( \sigma_\omega^2 \approx \frac{2}{12} \cdot \frac{\text{Resolution}^2}{(\Delta t)^2} \)

4. Quantifying Measurement Noise Variance

Using the encoder resolution \( \text{Resolution} = 0.0879^\circ = 0.001535 \, \text{radians} \):

\( \sigma_\theta^2 = \frac{(0.001535)^2}{12} \approx 1.96 \times 10^{-8} \, \text{rad}^2 \)

For a sampling interval \( \Delta t = 0.01 \, \text{seconds} \):

\( \sigma_\omega^2 \approx \frac{2}{12} \cdot \frac{(0.001535)^2}{(0.01)^2} \approx 1.96 \times 10^{-4} \, \text{rad}^2/\text{s}^2 \)
